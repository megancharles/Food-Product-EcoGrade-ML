Can ingredient lists predict a food product's environmental impact?

Life cycle assessment is the gold standard for measuring food's environmental footprint — but it's expensive, data-intensive, and slow. This research explores whether something far more accessible — the ingredient list — can do a similar job.

Agriculture and food production don't just emit greenhouse gases — they reshape the planet's land, water, and biodiversity at scale. And the impacts don't stop at the farm gate: packaging, transportation, storage, and waste all compound the footprint of every product on a supermarket shelf.

Ensuring everyone has access to a nutritious diet in a way that is environmentally sustainable is one of the largest challenges of our time. Addressing it requires tools that help producers measure their impact — and communicate it to consumers in ways that are actionable.

Eco-labelling and the green score

Eco-labels have emerged as the primary tool for communicating the environmental impact of food products to consumers. The most comprehensive approach currently being explored is the Green Score — a grading system from A+ to F that accounts for the full life cycle of a product.

The challenge: gathering the data to calculate these scores is hard.

  • 01 Food supply chains are complex — multiple stages, regions, and actors, from farms to distributors
  • 02 Many supply chain actors don't collect detailed environmental data, limiting transparency
  • 03 LCA requires numerous assumptions that vary by region, crop, and production method — introducing uncertainty
03

The research question

"Can food product ingredient list data be used to predict environmental performance — and if so, could it serve as a practical alternative to full life cycle assessment?"

Ingredient lists are already publicly available on almost every food product. If there is a meaningful statistical relationship between what a product contains and its environmental footprint, this could unlock a faster, cheaper, and more scalable route to eco-labelling — without waiting for complex LCA data to be gathered for every product.

This research uses Open Food Facts — an open-access, crowd-sourced database covering food products globally, including both ingredient lists and Green Score eco-grades — as a pre-labelled dataset to explore this relationship.

The approach treats ingredient data as a natural language / structured input and applies machine learning classification methods to predict eco-grade outcomes, examining how much predictive signal the ingredient list alone carries.
